In the furor against President Donald Trump‚Äôs ban on immigration, some are bringing up history. 
Did President Barack Obama also ban refugees from Iraq?

However, The Washington Post reported that a former Obama administration official named Jon Finer has ‚Äúdenied that any ban in Iraqi refugee admissions was put in place under Obama.‚Äù 
‚ÄúWhile the flow of Iraqi refugees slowed significantly during the Obama administration‚Äôs review, refugees continued to be admitted to the United States during that time, and there was not a single month in which no Iraqis arrived here,‚Äù Finer wrote in Foreign Policy . ‚ÄúIn other words, while there were delays in processing, there was no outright ban.‚Äù Another official backed up Finer‚Äôs account to the Post. 
The Post did reprint this 2011 exchange between Republican Senator from Maine, Susan Collins, and then Homeland Security Secretary Janet Napolitano as to whether ‚Äúa hold had been placed on Iraqi visa applications.‚Äù Collins asked at the congressional hearing: ‚ÄúSo my question is, is there a hold on that population until they can be more stringently vetted to ensure that we‚Äôre not letting into this country, people who would do us harm?‚Äù Napolitano responded,

Trump‚Äôs executive order stops all Iraqi citizens from temporarily entering the U.S. ‚Äúon any visa category,‚Äù affecting those trying to visit family or come here for work, in addition to live. It also affects six other countries. It originally included permanent legal residents of the U.S. with green cards, but the Department of Homeland Security later clarified that green card holders would be allowed into the country. 
While Obama did slow the refugee program, it did not impact green card holders, or anyone with a visa. It also did not affect refugees who had already gone through the vetting process. Trump‚Äôs travel ban initially barred entry to the United States to those groups, causing a chaotic situation for travelers from the seven affected countries and leading to protests at airports across the country. 
‚ÄúObama‚Äôs policy did not prevent all citizens of that country, including green-card holders, from traveling the United States. Trump‚Äôs policy is much more sweeping, though officials have appeared to pull back from barring permanent U.S. residents,‚Äù the Washington Post reports.

Trump‚Äôs order gives preference to religious minority groups, such as Christians, when the admissions resume, according to The New York Times . 
Obama didn‚Äôt give any preference to religious minority groups, and his action was limited to refugee resettlement (certainly, it didn‚Äôt affect green card holders, for example). However, Obama‚Äôs slowdown in Iraqi refugee admittance had consequences for some people and was driven by the terrorism case involving refugees, ABC News reported. The network reported that, as a result of the delays, an ‚ÄúIraqi who had aided American troops was assassinated before his refugee application could be processed, because of the immigration delays.‚Äù (One of the first men detained under Trump‚Äôs executive order, Hameed Darweesh , was an Iraqi interpreter for the U.S. military. Why did Obama‚Äôs administration slow processing the applications? 
It stemmed from the case out of Bowling Green, Kentucky. According to the Associated Press , ‚ÄúTwo Iraqi refugees plotted to send sniper rifles, Stinger missiles and money to al-Qaida operatives waging an insurgency back home against U.S. troops.‚Äù

ABC reported at the time, ‚ÄúSeveral dozen suspected terrorist bombmakers, including some believed to have targeted American troops, may have mistakenly been allowed to move to the United States as war refugees.‚Äù Among them, said ABC, was Alwan: ‚ÄúThe Iraqi had claimed to be a refugee who faced persecution back home,‚Äù but his fingerprints were found on a piece of cordless phone wired to a bomb. Trump‚Äôs immigration ban ‚Äì which has been met by intense criticism and protests at airports throughout the country ‚Äì temporarily stops immigration from six Muslim-majority countries: Iraq, Iran, Somalia, Sudan, Yemen, and Libya. A lawsuit has been filed asking the courts to deem the entire Trump executive order unconstitutional under the 5th Amendment, contending it subjects Muslims to disparate treatment. The Trump order had also affected people with green cards and those visiting America to see family or for work; there is a provision for case-by-case exemption in some circumstances. There have been reports of students, professors, and others stuck overseas or at airports who had lived in the United States for years.

The New York Times reports that about the same number of Christians and Muslims were admitted to the U.S. as refugees in 2016, despite Trump‚Äôs claims that Christians had basically no chance to get asylum under President Obama.

So what‚Äôs the difference with Trump‚Äôs action? 
First, Obama responded to an actual threat ‚Äî the discovery that two Iraqi refugees had been implicated in bombmaking in Iraq that had targeted U.S. troops. (Iraq, after all, was a war zone.) Trump, by contrast, issued his executive order without any known triggering threat. (His staff has pointed to attacks unrelated to the countries named in his order.) 
Second, Obama did not announce a ban on visa applications. In fact, as seen in Napolitano‚Äôs answer to Collins, administration officials danced around that question. There was certainly a lot of news reporting that visa applications had slowed to a trickle. But the Obama administration never said it had a policy to halt all applications. Indeed, it is now clear that no ban was put in place.
